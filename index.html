Pythagoras

" There is not a single detail in the life of Pythagoras that stands uncontradicted. But it is possible, from a more or less critical selection of the data, to construct a plausible account. "


--- Walter Burkert, German scholar of Greek mythology, 1972

Pythagoras of Samos (Ancient Greek: Πυθαγόρας; c. 570 - c. 495 BC) was an ancient Ionian Greek philosopher, polymath and the eponymous founder of Pythagoreanism. His political and religious teachings were well known in Magna Graecia and influenced the philosophies of Plato, Aristotle, and, through them, the West in general. Knowledge of his life is clouded by legend; modern scholars disagree regarding Pythagoras's education and influences, but they do agree that, around 530 BC, he travelled to Croton in southern Italy, where he founded a school in which initiates were sworn to secrecy and lived a communal, ascetic lifestyle. In antiquity, Pythagoras was credited with many mathematical and scientific discoveries including

  • the Pythagorean theorem
  • Pythagorean tuning
  • the five regular solids
  • the Theory of Proportions
  • the sphericity of the Earth
  • the identity of the morning and evening stars as the planet Venus.
It was said that he was the first man to call himself a philosopher ("lover of wisdom")[c] and that he was the first to divide the globe into five climatic zones. Classical historians debate whether Pythagoras made these discoveries, and many of the accomplishments credited to him likely originated earlier or were made by his colleagues or successors. Some accounts mention that the philosophy associated with Pythagoras was related to mathematics and that numbers were important, but it is debated to what extent, if at all, he actually contributed to mathematics or natural philosophy


Life

  1. Early Life (c. 570-530 BCE)

    • Born on the Greek island of Samos
    • Father: Mnesarchus (merchant)
    • Mother: Pythais
    • Showed early mathematical talent

  2. Travel and Education (c. 530-520 BCE)

    • Traveled to Egypt, Babylon, and India to study mathematics, astronomy, and philosophy
    • Influenced by Egyptian and Babylonian mathematics
    • Learned from renowned scholars, including Pherecydes of Syros

  3. Founding of the Pythagorean School (c. 520-500 BCE)

    • Returned to Samos, then moved to Croton, Italy
    • Founded a philosophical and mathematical school
    • Attracted followers, including philosophers, mathematicians, and politicians

  4. Later Life and Legacy (c. 500-495 BCE)

    • Faced opposition and persecution from rival philosophers and politicians
    • Moved to Metapontum, Italy, where he died
    • Legacy continued through his followers, influencing Western philosophy and mathematics

Contributions

  1. Mathematics

    • Pythagorean Theorem (a^2 + b^2 = c^2): Relates the lengths of a right-angled triangle's sides.
    • Irrational Numbers: Discovered that some numbers cannot be expressed as simple fractions.
    • Geometry: Studied properties of triangles, quadrilaterals, and polygons.
    • Number Theory: Explored properties of numbers, including even/odd, prime, and perfect numbers.

  2. Music Theory

    • Harmonics: Discovered mathematical relationships between musical notes and intervals
    • Musical Scales: Developed the Pythagorean scale, still used today.
    • Instrument Design: Created the monochord, a single-stringed instrument.

  3. Astronomy

    • Heliocentric Model: Proposed that Earth and other planets orbit the Sun
    • Spherical Earth: Argued that Earth is a sphere, contradicting flat Earth theories.
    • Planetary Motion: Studied and predicted planetary movements.

  4. Philosophy

    • Dualism: Introduced the concept of opposing forces (e.g., good/evil, light/dark).
    • Transmigration of Souls: Believed in reincarnation and the immortality of the soul.
    • Harmony and Balance: Advocated for living in harmony with nature and achieving balance within oneself.

  5. Science

    • Concept of Unity: Proposed that the universe is interconnected and governed by mathematical laws
    • Five Regular Solids: Identified and studied the properties of the five Platonic solids.
    • Acoustics: Investigated the physics of sound and vibration.


Pythagoras' Theorem


" In a right-angled triangle, the sum of the areas of the square made along the sides which are perpendicular to each other is equal to the area of the square made along the longest side. "

Pythagoras' Theorem is a fundamental concept in geometry that describes the relationship between the lengths of the sides of a right-angled triangle. The theorem states:

a² + b² = c²

where:
  • a and b are the lengths of the two sides that form the right angle (legs)
  • c is the length of the hypotenuse (the side opposite the right angle)

Key Points:

  • The theorem only applies to right-angled triangles.
  • The hypotenuse (c) is always the longest side.
  • The theorem can be used to find the length of the hypotenuse (c) or one of the legs (a or b).

Example:
Find the length of the hypotenuse (c) in a right-angled triangle with legs of 3 inches (a) and 4 inches (b).
Solution:

  • a² + b² = c²
  • 3² + 4² = c²
  • 9 + 16 = c²
  • 25 = c²
  • c = √25
  • c = 5 inches
